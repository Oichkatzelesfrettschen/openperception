Abstract

Blue cone monochromacy (BCM) is a congenital vision disorder characterized by complete loss or severely reduced long- and middle-wavelength cone function, caused by mutations in the OPN1LW/OPN1MW gene cluster on the X-chromosome. BCM patients typically suffer from poor visual acuity, severely impaired color discrimination, myopia, and nystagmus. In this review, we cover the genetic causes of BCM, clinical features of BCM patients, genetic testing, and clinical outcome measurements for future BCM clinical trials. However, our emphasis is on detailing the animal models for BCM and gene therapy using adeno-associated vectors (AAV). We describe two mouse models resembling the two most common causes of BCM, current progress in proof-of-concept studies to treat BCM with deletion mutations, the challenges we face, and future directions.

Keywords: blue cone monochromacy, cone opsin, cone dystrophy, AAV, gene therapy, opsin knockout mice, OPN1MW, OPN1LW

INTRODUCTION

Cone photoreceptors are responsible for our high acuity, daylight, and color vision. In the human retina, three different cone opsin proteins (S-opsin, M-opsin, L-opsin) are expressed, with only one type expressed per cone. Similar to rhodopsin in rods, cone opsins are crucial in both the maintenance and structural integrity of the cone outer segment, the light-sensing organelle of the cell. Additionally, these proteins are required for initiation of phototransduction and overall visual function in cones 1–6. S-cones express S- (blue) opsin, which is encoded by the OPN1SW gene located on chromosome 7, and are sensitive to short-wavelength light. S-cones comprise only 2–7% of cones, with the majority localized to the peripheral retina. M- (green) and L- (red) cones (sensitive to medium- and long-wavelength light, respectively) comprise ~95% of all cones and are concentrated in the fovea, the structure at the central retina which is responsible for high visual acuity1–3, 7. X-linked retinal diseases resulting from mutations in the L- (OPN1LW, OMIM *300822) and M- (OPN1MW, OMIM *300821) opsin gene cluster are associated with a wide range of visual defects, including red/green color vision deficiency, X-linked cone dystrophy/dysfunction, high myopia with abnormal cone function, and blue cone monochromacy (BCM) 8–22. BCM (OMIM #303700) is an X-linked cone dysfunction syndrome that results in complete loss or severe reduction of L- and M- cone function. It is estimated that BCM affects approximately 1 in 100,000 people worldwide 23, 24. In this review, we focus on the current status of BCM gene therapy research, including investigation in animal models designed to resemble the retinas of BCM patients, as well as the future directions and challenges associated with BCM gene therapy. We will also discuss the genetic causes and clinical features of BCM, genetic testing, and pertinent clinical trial outcome measurements for patients with BCM.

GENETIC CAUSE OF BCM

L- and M-opsin are encoded by OPN1LW and OPN1MW, respectively, which are arranged in a head-to-tail tandem array on the X-chromosome (Xq28). While there is tremendous variation among people in terms of copy numbers of OPN1LW and OPN1MW in the human genome, on average, one copy of OPN1LW is present at the proximal position of the opsin gene array, followed by one or more copies of OPN1MW 25, 26. Expression of these genes is driven and regulated by their own specific promoters, as well as an upstream cis-element termed the locus control region (LCR). Previous studies demonstrate that the LCR ensures only the first two opsin genes are expressed and that each cone only expresses one type of opsin 4–6. However, there is conflicting evidence on the expression of “only” the first two genes in the array 27. OPN1LW and OPN1MW are thought to be duplicates, as these genes share 96% sequence homology. The sequence variation between these genes is thought to be involved in the spectral tuning of the specific opsin proteins 28.

Due to their high degree of homology and physical proximity, unequal homologous recombination and gene conversion events between OPN1LW and OPN1MW are common 13, 26, 29. This nonhomologous recombination often results in formation of hybrid genes which contain portions of both OPN1LW and OPN1MW genes 19, 30, 31. Alterations or deficiencies in proper red/green color vision may vary between individuals depending on the hybrid gene patterns created from these events. Three main mechanisms are thought to cause BCM. The first results in the formation of large deletions most frequently covering the LCR and parts of or the entire cone opsin gene array that abolish the expression of both OPN1LW and OPN1MW. The second involves a two-step process. In the first step, non-allelic homologous recombination occurs, resulting in a single OPN1LW or single OPN1LW/OPN1MW hybrid gene. In the second step, an additional deletion or missense mutation occurs, leading to inactivation of functional L- or M-opsin. The most common missense mutation observed in this scenario is a Cys203Arg (C203R) mutation in an OPN1LW/OPN1MW hybrid gene 4, 12, 19, 20, 32–34. The third mechanism thought to cause BCM involves rare OPN1LW/OPN1MW interchange haplotypes within exon 3 in polymorphic positions. The most common disease-causing haplotypes have been identified as LIAVA, LVAVA, MIAVA, and LIAVS. The single-letter code indicates the amino acids expressed at each variant position (p.153, p.171, p.174, p.178, and p.180) encoded within exon 3 35. It has been shown that LIAVA, LVAVA, MIAVA, and LIAVS variants affect vision primarily through abnormal splicing, rather than abnormal protein structure or function 33, 36–38

CLINICAL FEATURES OF BCM

BCM patients typically display severely reduced visual acuity and impaired color vision from birth. Patients also often show signs of photophobia and present with infantile nystagmus, as well as myopia 12, 17. Many of these symptoms are also hallmarks of achromatopsia, presenting a difficult challenge for clinicians to distinguish between the two disorders. Pedigree analysis is typically required to properly diagnose patients with these defects as BCM shows an X-linked inheritance pattern, while achromatopsia is an autosomal recessive disease affecting males and females equally. BCM patients have a normal to mildly subnormal dark-adapted rod electroretinogram (ERG). Furthermore, while the S-cone ERG of a BCM patient is normal, 30Hz flicker ERG responses mediated by L- and M- cones are undetectable 8, 39, 40. Fundus examination is also usually normal, however, retinal pigment epithelial (RPE) atrophy at the macula may be noted in older patients 8, 17, 41–43.

Spectral-domain OCT (SD-OCT) is used widely in clinical settings for visualization of the retinal layers as distinct reflective bands. The second hyperreflective outer retinal band, commonly refer to as ellipsoid zone (EZ), is the junction between the inner and outer segment of photoreceptors, has been shown to correspond to photoreceptor integrity. Discontinuities in the EZ have been observed at the fovea in BCM patients, suggesting disruption of cone photoreceptor structure 43–46. Retinal cross-sections of BCM patients imaged by OCT also show thinning of the foveal outer nuclear layer (ONL) at an early age 43, 44, 46. At the foveola, the center of the fovea which is dominated by L- and M- cones, the ONL is abnormally thin in almost all patients. Younger BCM patients between the ages of 5 and 20 present with a mean foveolar ONL thickness that is about 60% of normal. Longitudinal OCT data suggest there is a slow and progressive thinning of the ONL at the foveola with age 43. (Fig. 1, adapted from the original article by Cideciyan 2012), and some patients display macular atrophy at later ages 8, 15. OCT images also demonstrate that remaining foveal cones of BCM patients have significantly shortened outer segments but there is no observed correlation between cone outer segment length and age (Fig. 2, adapted from the original article by Cideciyan 2012) 43.

SD-OCT studies have also been carried out to compare both foveal hypoplasia (lack of full development of the morphology of the fovea) and the appearance of the EZ at the fovea between patients with achromatopsia or BCM 47. The incidence of foveal hypoplasia was significantly more prevalent in patients with achromatopsia compared to those with BCM. There was also a significant difference in the distribution of EZ grades between the achromatopsia and BCM, with grade II EZ (EZ disruption) being by far the most common phenotype in BCM, while the achromatopsia patients exhibit a relatively equal prevalence of EZ grades I (continuous EZ), II, and IV (presence of a hyporeflective zone). In addition to electrophysiological testing and SD-OCT, psychophysical testing using the Berson color plates can be a useful tool in distinguishing BCM from achromatopsia. In this color vision test, a patient receives two instructional and four test plates. The test plates contain four arrows, three of which are blue-green and one that is purple-blue. Patients with BCM are easily able to decipher which arrow is purple-blue, while those with achromatopsia are unable to determine this arrow from those that are blue-green 41.

Adaptive optics scanning light ophthalmoscopy (AOSLO) allows non-invasive visualization of the living human retina with single-cell resolution. It has the capability to show cell mosaics of the healthy and diseased retina 48. In comparison to SD-OCT, AOSLO allows to define the identity and spatial density of photoreceptors. Healthy cone photoreceptors are the most easily visualized structures in confocal AOSLO images, appearing as larger bright spots that have a central reflective core surrounded by a darker ring, and they are surrounded by smaller structures that are rods 48, 49. In BCM patients, two types of cones were identified using AOSLO. The first included normal cones that retain a central reflective core representing the S-cone population. The second type includes dark cones which are missing the central reflective component, representing remnant L- or M- cones with intact inner segments but significantly shortened or missing outer segments 24, 43, 45.

To correlate the level of severity of the BCM phenotype with genotype, the structure of cone photoreceptors was compared between patients with the two most common causes of BCM, those with either A) large deletion mutations or B) a C203R missense mutation in the OPN1LW/OPN1MW gene cluster. It was found that both genotypes have a population of non-waveguiding photoreceptors, although the spectral identity and level of function remain unknown 24. Another study showed that patients with a C203R mutation have decades longer preservation of foveal cone outer nuclear layer thickness and a slower rate of development of inner/outer segment defects 46. Since mutant C203R protein is misfolded, it was postulated to be toxic to cone photoreceptors, similar to what has been observed for known rhodopsin missense mutations that cause dominant retinitis pigmentosa. Therefore, it was unexpected that the C203R genotype appeared less aggressive compared to patients with deletion mutations. However, preliminary research in our laboratory shows that cones in the corresponding C198R mouse model do not seem to degenerate faster than those in the deletion mouse model, consistent with the findings in the BCM patients 50.

In summary, the AOSLO imaging results, taken together with OCT, provided strong evidence that BCM patients have a lower cone density than those seen in normal subjects, but a higher total cone density than would be expected for just the S-cones, suggesting the existence of a remnant L-/M-cone population in BCM.

GENETIC TESTING

After clinical diagnosis of BCM, genetic testing is required to confirm the diagnosis and determine the exact mutation in order to identify any potential gene therapies. Typically, BCM genetic testing is carried out by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) or long-range PCR followed by Sanger sequencing of the LCR and all coding exons. Due to the complex structure of the locus control region (LCR) and the presence of highly homologous sequences between the OPN1LW and OPN1MW genes, overlapping primers are commonly used to investigate the structure and integrity of the LCR, as well as the OPN1LW/OPN1MW gene cluster. Restriction fragment length polymorphism (RFLP) is used to determine the presence or absence of a common point mutation. For example, RFLP in exon 4 via BstUI digestion is used to analyze for the common point mutation c.607T > C p.C203R, while the RFLP in exon 5 via RsaI differentiates the dimorphism at amino acid position 277, wherein p.Y277 is specific for the OPN1LW gene and p.F277 is specific for the OPN1MW gene 43. Additionally, genome walking and inverse PCR are used to identify the precise junction breaking points 12, 13, 33, 43, 51, 52, and quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) is used to estimate the relative ratio of the first L-opsin gene versus the downstream M-opsin genes in the opsin gene arrays 53.

Recently, a new genetic assay for the analysis of the OPN1LW/OPN1MW gene cluster was developed which detects both structural and nucleotide variants. It consists of copy number analysis via multiplex ligation-dependent probe amplification and sequence analysis via long-read circular consensus sequencing 31. Additionally, microarray-based techniques have also been used in diagnosing BCM 54. Unfortunately, routine short read next generation sequencing either applying gene panels or doing whole exome sequencing actually perform rather bad for the cone opsin gene array. Moreover, microarray-based testing usually lack resolution and cannot resolve gene array order, and thus may only be applicable for the detection of some of the deletions.

GENE THERAPY for BCM

Improved genetic diagnosis of inherited retinal diseases and advances in retinal gene therapy have led to great promise in treating different forms of inherited blindness. Due to its immunoprivileged environment, direct accessibility, and the number of established noninvasive techniques that can be employed for monitoring retinal structure and assessing visual function after surgery, the retina is a favorable target for administration of gene therapy 55, 56. Generally, retinal gene therapy is carried out by a subretinal or intravitreal injection of a therapeutic reagent packaged within a viral vector, commonly an adeno-associated virus (AAV) or lentivirus. Two different strategies are often used to combat inherited blinding diseases by gene therapy, including 1) gene augmentation, which delivers a functional copy of a gene to restore expression of a mutated gene, and 2) gene-editing, which is designed to directly edit and correct the mutation. Currently, BCM gene therapy research has focused on gene augmentation in a mouse model mimicking large deletion mutations and has shown success. Furthermore, a study which used gene augmentation to treat color blindness in adult monkeys with dichromatic vision suggests that the adult primate’s visual cortex can process new cone inputs upon expression of a third cone opsin with different wavelength sensitivity 57. Together, these data provide compelling evidence that the visual cortex of BCM patients may respond to new chromatic inputs similarly following treatment.

Retinal structure of BCM patients

Retinal cross-sections of BCM patients imaged by optical coherence tomography (OCT) show disruption of EZ and thinning of the foveal outer nuclear layer (ONL) at an early age 43, 44, 46, 58. At the foveola, the center of the fovea dominated by L- and M- cones, the ONL was abnormally thin in almost all patients. Younger BCM patients between the ages of 5 and 20 present with a mean foveolar ONL thickness that is about 60% of normal. Longitudinal OCT data suggest there is a slow and progressive thinning of the ONL at the foveola with age 43, and some patients display macular atrophy at later ages 8, 15. OCT images also demonstrate that any remaining foveal cones of BCM patients have significantly shortened outer segments and there is no correlation between cone outer segment length and age 43. Adaptive optics scanning laser ophthalmoscope (AOSLO) imaging shows a disrupted cone mosaic and EZ in BCM patients which further confirms the reduction of foveal cones 24, 45, 58, 59.

Detailed studies on the fate of opsin-deficient cone photoreceptors and their structural changes have been carried out in BCM patients with deletion mutations or a C203R missense mutation 24, 43, 46. Interestingly, although patients with a C203R mutation exhibited decades-longer persistence of foveal photoreceptor outer nuclear layer thickness and a slower rate of development of inner and outer segment defects compared to patients with large deletion mutations, all patients had comparably severe losses of the foveal structure at late ages.

AAV-mediated gene therapy for BCM with deletion mutations

Currently, the majority of investigations into gene therapy for BCM have been carried out using adeno associated virus (AAV) as a vector in mouse models of deletion mutations. There are several differences between human and murine cones in terms of anatomy, distribution, and opsin expression, so knowledge of these variances is critical for assessing the pros and cons of using a mouse model for BCM gene therapy research. As stated previously, the human retina contains three different types of cones: L-, M- and S-cones, with each cone expressing only one type of opsin 1, 2, 7, 60. In contrast, the murine retina only harbors M- and S-cones, with most cones co-expressing both opsins. Where L- and M-cones are concentrated in fovea of the human retina, mice do not possess a fovea and their cones are distributed across the entire retina with a dorsal-to-ventral gradient. S-opsin predominates the ventral retina and M-opsin is expressed across the entire retina with its highest concentration in the dorsal retina 61, 62.

Initially, M-opsin knockout mice (Opn1mw−/−) were used to study gene therapy in BCM patients with deletion mutations 63. In this mouse model, M-opsin is absent from both the dorsal and ventral retina, with S-opsin being predominately detected in the ventral region, similar to wild-type mice. Peanut agglutinin (PNA) was used to assess Opn1mw−/− cone viability, as it is a lectin that specifically binds to the cone sheath – the extracellular glycoprotein matrix secreted by cone inner segments. PNA staining of Opn1mw−/− retinal cross-sections revealed an intact cone sheath in both the dorsal and ventral regions of Opn1mw−/− retinas, suggesting that dorsal cones remain viable in the absence of M-opsin. Due to loss of M-opsin, Opn1mw−/− mice do not possess middle-wavelength M-cone ERG response, but do present with a normal S-cone ERG. Furthermore, when cone outer segment morphology of Opn1mw−/− mice was examined using immunostaining with antibodies against the cone-specific phosphodiesterase 6α’ subunit (PDE6α’) and cone transducin α-subunit (cTα), PDE6α’ staining was primarily detected in cone inner segments and cTα expression was completely absent. These data suggest that Opn1mw−/− dorsal cones, where M- opsin is absent and S- opsin expression is low, do not form proper outer segments, similar to the morphologies of L- and M-cones observed in the human fovea of BCM patients 64. Gene replacement therapy using AAV5-mediated expression of mouse Opn1mw, driven by the cone-specific promoter PR2.1, demonstrated that treated eyes gained robust M-cone function when analyzed by ERG 63. AAV5-mediated expression of human OPN1LW or OPN1MW also promoted proper dorsal cone outer segment morphogenesis, restoring expression and normal localization of both PDE6α’ and cTα to the outer segment 64. Together, these results provided an experimental basis for BCM gene therapy clinical trials using an AAV-mediated gene replacement approach.

Although the Opn1m−/− mouse is a working disease model for BCM patients with deletion mutations, this model has its limitations. As mentioned earlier, each human cone expresses only one type of opsin – L-, M-, or S- opsin. Due to this, affected L- and M-cones in the BCM retina do not express any functional opsin proteins. In contrast, as low levels of endogenous S-opsin remain expressed in dorsal Opn1m−/− cones, these photoreceptors may not reflect the true pathophysiology of BCM cones. Moreover, as Opn1m−/− retinas contain a large number of S-cones with normal S-cone structure and function 63, AAV-mediated expression of either human L/M-opsin or mouse M-opsin in these S-cones generates additional long-/middle- wavelength ERG signals which interfere with interpretation of the gene therapy outcome. To circumvent this issue, Opn1mw−/−/Opn1sw−/− mice (DKO mice) were generated which exhibit complete ablation of M- and S- opsin expression in all cone photoreceptors 65. While the DKO mouse could also be a model for achromatopsia, the fact that it lacks all mouse cone visual pigment also better mimics BCM cones at the cellular level.

Cones across the entire DKO retina present with shortened outer segments, structurally resembling cones in the BCM fovea. As DKO cones do not possess cone opsins, these mice lack photopic ERG responses, ensuring that observed increases in visual function are a direct reflection of functional rescue due to gene therapy. It was shown that cones within the DKO retina remain viable at younger ages but subsequently undergo degeneration. By 6 months of age, the numbers of viable cones in Opn1mw−/−/Opn1sw−/− retinas are reduced by half 65. AAV5-mediated expression of human OPN1LW under a PR2.1 promoter was used to treat Opn1mw−/− mice before 2 months of age. At 1 month post-injection, the photopic b-wave maximum amplitude measured by ERG was ~60% of wild-type, while contralateral untreated control eyes showed no photopic ERG response (Fig. 3). Treatment also restored cone outer segments as well as expression of cone outer segment-specific proteins such as PDE6α’ and cTα (Fig. 4). Rescue persisted long-term, at least 8-months post-injection. Furthermore, optomotor analysis showed that cone-mediated visual behavior was similar to wild-type thresholds at 8-months post-treatment. However, when mice were treated at 5- or 7- months of age, the chance and degree of rescue was significantly reduced, despite retention of viable cones in the retina, demonstrating that early intervention may be crucial for efficient and stable rescue (Fig. 3 & 4).

AAV-mediated gene therapy for BCM with point mutations

Presence of a C203R missense mutation in a single OPN1LW/MW hybrid gene or in multiple OPN1LW/MW genes is one of the most common causes of BCM 19. Opn1mwC198ROpn1sw−/− mice were generated to model human BCM with the corresponding C203R mutation 50. Based on studies of an important disulfide bond between C110 and C187 in rhodopsin 66, it is postulated that the cone opsin equivalent C203 is involved in disulfide bond formation and that a C203R mutation disrupts this bond, leading to improper cone pigment tertiary structure that may inhibit proper transport to the outer segment. In fact, it has been shown that the C187Y mutation in rhodopsin leads to early and severe autosomal dominant retinitis pigmentosa 67. Studies also demonstrate that C203R expressed in vitro is retained in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) due to improper folding 32. Similar to the retinal phenotype displayed in DKO mice, cones of Opn1mwC198ROpn1sw−/− mice degenerate with age, with absent or significantly shortened cone outer segments 50. These phenotypes are similar to those observed in BCM patients with C203R mutations, demonstrating that this model can be used to investigate the disease mechanism of BCM and in the development of gene therapy. Gene therapy in Opn1mwC198ROpn1sw−/− mice is currently under development in our lab.

Besides C203R, several other missense mutations in the OPN1MW/OPN1LW gene cluster have been linked to BCM, including W177R 13, P307L 68, K82E 69, M273K 70, and R247X 20. Each of these mutations has various effects on the folding and activity of the opsin protein. P307L opsin is glycosylated and transported to the cone outer segment, yet light absorption at the appropriate wavelength and subsequent phototransduction is compromised. This mutation is predicted to affect the structure of the binding pocket for 11-cis-retinal 68, 71. The W177R mutation and its equivalent W161R mutation in rod opsin both result in protein misfolding and ER retention when expressed in vitro 13, similar to the phenotype displayed by the C203R mutant. W177R mutant expressed via AAV in Opn1mw−/− mice was detected exclusively in the cone inner segment and ER, suggesting that it is also misfolded in vivo 71. An R247X mutation results in an early termination codon and a truncated protein. The underlying disease mechanisms of these cone opsin mutants all appear to be different, with some genotypes causing potential dominant negative phenotypes, signifying the need for development of accurate animal models of each BCM genotype. Generation of these models will allow for proper investigation and development of gene therapy strategies, including those which incorporate gene editing or gene silencing.

Gene Therapy Applicability from Mouse Models to Human Patients

Although gene therapy has shown efficacy in mouse models of BCM, there are two issues that need to be addressed in terms of applicability to human patients. First, murine retinas do not contain foveae and their cones are spread evenly across the entire retina. Secondly, each human cone expresses only one type of opsin, while the majority of cones in the mouse retina co-express both M- and S-opsin. Recently, our lab has addressed these concerns by crossing Opn1mw−/−/Opn1sw−/− mice with Nr−/− mice 72–74 to generate mice with all-cone retinas lacking opsin expression. Preliminary data show that Nrl−/−/Opn1mw−/−/Opn1sw−/− all-cone retinas lack opsin expression, have significantly shortened outer segments, and lack photopic M-cone ERG responses, thereby structurally and functionally mimicking the foveae of BCM patients with deletion mutations. Studies are currently underway in our lab to determine the effectiveness of gene replacement therapy in this opsin-lacking all-cone mouse model.

As L- and M-cone function is abolished in BCM patients, another question regarding BCM gene therapy is whether BCM patients should be treated with an AAV expressing M- and L-opsin together or separately. We have shown previously that when treated with AAV vectors containing an equal mixture of OPN1LW and OPN1MW in the Opn1mw−/− retinas, most of the cones co-expressed both opsins 64. However, there was great variation in terms of the total amount of opsin being expressed and the OPN1LW/OPN1MW ratio among even adjacent treated cones. This is expected due to the stochastic nature of expression from AAV vectors. Since human cones normally express only one type of opsin, we do not know how the visual cortex in BCM patients will respond if two different opsins are co-expressed in a single cone. In our opinion, improving visual acuity is more valuable to BCM patients rather than restoring complete color vision. If we aim to ensure that the most important treatment outcome of BCM gene therapy is improvement of visual acuity, it seems the best plan of action would be to treat BCM patients with just one opsin gene, thereby eliminating any biological variations or uncertainties from treating with two opsins and to simplify the interpretation of study results. Currently, it is thought that between L- and M-opsin, L-opsin may serve as a better candidate for gene replacement therapy because it would give rise to a wider spectrum of perceptible light frequencies in combination with endogenous S-cone frequencies compared to M- and S-opsin combined. Moreover, in the human retina, L-cones are more abundant than M-cones 75, 76.

OUTCOME MEASUREMENT CONSIDERATIONS FOR FUTURE BCM GENE THERAPY CLINICAL TRIALS

Several studies have explored tailored outcome measurements in order to account for the unique features of BCM retinas and the preferred intravitreal injection route suggested by clinicians. As light perception in BCM patients is dominated by both rods and S-cones, and as rods have been shown to mediate a degree of daylight vision in BCM patients, it is crucial for a clear outcome measurement to be established to differentiate small cone-driven gain from the native rod function 77, 78. There is a possibility that initial AAV gene therapy trials will achieve only minor increases in cone-driven function, making it possible that assessment of visual acuity and full-field ERG would serve as inappropriate outcome measurements as they are not sensitive enough to detect focal changes in retinal function. Of course, the primary outcome measurement of any interventional study – including BCM gene therapy – should be safety parameters, including routine ophthalmic examination and OCT, in order to monitor for systemic and ocular toxicity 79. OCT is also a useful tool for pre-enrollment imaging to select for candidates with a detectable foveal outer nuclear layer as it indicates a structural integrity amenable for gene therapy 43, 46.

It has been shown that chromatic microperimetry could be used as an efficacy outcome measurement because BCM patients demonstrate a large loss of sensitivity to red stimuli presented on a cyan adapting background at the fovea and surrounding parafovea 77. Additionally, a Nidek MP1 microperimeter was modified and developed into a more sensitive outcome measurement for detection of small improvements in L/M-cone function as this modification generates varied stimuli of a very wide range of luminance and chromaticity, granting precise spatiotemporal control locked to retinal features 80. Researchers continue to work to develop and test new methods which can be used as outcome measurement of BCM gene therapy in clinical trials. For example, use of an MNREAD acuity chart has shown that BCM patients exhibit abnormal reading performance in all parameters examined (reading speed, critical print size, reading acuity, and the reading accessibility index), demonstrating that MNREAD measures of reading performance would be a robust secondary outcome in a clinical trial protocol 81. More recently, two color arrangement tests (D-15 and Roth-28) derived from the Farnsworth-Munsell (FM) 100 Hue and the Color Assessment and Diagnosis (CAD) test were utilized to evaluate patients with BCM, providing promising new tools to assess color vision in BCM clinical trials 82.

As mentioned previously, BCM patients share many similar clinical features as patients with achromatopsia, such as reduced visual acuity, impaired color vision, photophobia, and nystagmus. They also share similar retinal phenotypes, including foveal hypoplasia and disruption of the EZ zone. Therefore, many of the outcome measurements used in achromatopsia gene therapy clinical trials can be referenced for outcome measurements in BCM gene therapy trials. As shown by a clinical trial study for CNGA3 gene therapy 83, safety was the primary end point, which was assessed by clinical examination of ocular inflammation. Systemic safety was evaluated by vital signs, routine clinical chemistry testing, and full and differential blood cell counts. Visual function and patient reported outcomes served as secondary outcome measurements. Visual function reports the number of participants with improved visual function measured by best corrected visual acuity, contrast sensitivity, color vision, temporal resolution, retinal sensitivity, pupillography, and the evaluation of retinal imaging. Patient reported outcomes include patients who are able to demonstrate functional gain associated with improvements in the ability to identify letters and numbers, as well as in color vision using the validated Visual Function Questionnaire 25 test 84. Together, these outcome measurements all can be potentially useful for assessing efficacy of a BCM clinical trial.

SUMMARY and FUTURE DIRECTIONS

One critical observation from gene therapy in DKO mice is that rescue efficacy is significantly reduced when mice were treated at older ages (5 months and older), although more than 50% of cones are still present 65. In order to extend the therapeutic window, mechanisms of cone degeneration in BCM must be elucidated. Secondly, as current studies restrict the assessment of structural cone rescue to fluorescent imaging of outer segment markers, additional investigation of cone ultrastructure through methods such as transmission electron microscopy is vital. Finally, as subretinal injections require retinal detachment for proper transduction of the photoreceptors, successful gene therapy for primary cone degenerative disorders like BCM via subretinal vector injection would require foveal detachment. This poses a serious safety issue, as previous clinical trials have shown loss of foveal cone outer segments and cone death following foveal detachment from subretinal injections 79, 85, 86. Alternatively, intravitreal injections would be a much safer method of vector administration as they do not require retinal detachment, are less invasive, and can be performed in an outpatient setting. In order to achieve effective intravitreal injection, future studies must focus on improving AAV vectors to exhibit increased vector penetrance, transduction efficiency, and transgene expression.
