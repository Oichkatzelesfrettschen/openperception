## Recent Academic Papers (2023-2025)

### 2025 Publications

#### The Human Photosensitive Epilepsy Model (Part 1)

- **Title:** The human photosensitive epilepsy model for clinical proof-of-principle trials of novel antiseizure medications. 1. Use of the EEG in drug development and characteristics of the model
- **Authors:** Kasteleijn-Nolst Trenite, Dorothee; et al.
- **Year:** 2025
- **Journal:** Epilepsia
- **PMID:** 40411480
- **URL:** https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/epi.18468
- **Description:** Review describing how clinical development of novel antiseizure medications (ASMs) would benefit from an early proof-of-principle (POP) model. The photosensitivity model uses the photoparoxysmal electroencephalography (EEG) response (PPR) as a surrogate for seizures.

#### The Human Photosensitive Epilepsy Model (Part 2)

- **Title:** The human photosensitive epilepsy model for clinical proof-of-principle trials of novel antiseizure medications: 2. Analysis of drug trials and predictive value of the model
- **Authors:** Loscher, Wolfgang; Kasteleijn-Nolst Trenite, Dorothee
- **Year:** 2025
- **Journal:** Epilepsia
- **PMID:** 40347110
- **DOI:** 10.1111/epi.18444
- **URL:** https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/epi.18444
- **PMC:** PMC12371661
- **Description:** Analysis of drugs categorized into three groups: drugs that suppressed PPR, drugs that exerted no effect on PPR, and drugs that increased PPR (indicating proconvulsant effect). For the vast majority of drugs, the model correctly predicted activity during long-term studies.

#### Evaluating Conformance of Video Safety Tools

- **Title:** Evaluating Conformance of Video Safety Tools for Photosensitive Epilepsy
- **Year:** 2025
- **Journal:** Universal Access in Human-Computer Interaction
- **Pages:** 15780:85-98
- **Description:** Evaluation of video safety tools for detecting photosensitive epilepsy triggers in video content.

## Pattern-Induced Seizures Research

### Foundational Research

#### Epilepsy Foundation Working Group Review

- **Title:** Photic- and pattern-induced seizures: a review for the Epilepsy Foundation of America Working Group
- **PMID:** 16146439
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16146439/
- **PDF:** https://www.researchgate.net/publication/7615895_Photic-_and_Pattern-induced_Seizures_A_Review_for_the_Epilepsy_Foundation_of_America_Working_Group
- **Description:** Comprehensive review establishing that photosensitivity occurs in approximately 0.3-3% of the population. Frequencies of 15-25 Hz are most provocative, with range extending from 1-65 Hz.

#### Epilepsy Foundation Expert Consensus

- **Title:** Photic- and Pattern-induced Seizures: Expert Consensus of the Epilepsy Foundation of America Working Group
- **PMID:** 16146438
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16146438/
- **PDF:** https://www.researchgate.net/publication/7615894_Photic-_and_Pattern-induced_Seizures_Expert_Consensus_of_the_Epilepsy_Foundation_of_America_Working_Group
- **Description:** Expert consensus on pattern thresholds: when stripes subtend >0.006 steradians at minimal viewing distance, with luminance >50 cd/m2, pattern should display no more than five light-dark pairs if oscillating/flashing.

### Pattern Sensitivity Studies

#### Self-Induction Behavior

- **Title:** Pattern-sensitive patients with epilepsy use uncomfortable visual stimuli to self-induce seizures
- **Year:** 2021
- **PMID:** 34252828
- **URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1525505021004509
- **Description:** Study of self-induction behavior in pattern-sensitive epilepsy patients. Found patients describe visual triggers as "uncomfortable" but enjoyable visual epileptic symptoms transform uncomfortable images into pleasant stimuli.

#### Pattern Sensitivity in Children

- **Title:** Pattern sensitivity and photosensitivity in epileptic children with visually induced seizures
- **PMID:** 8082632
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8082632/
- **Description:** Study finding 51% of epileptic children showed sensitivity to both light and pattern, 33% showed photosensitivity alone, and 16% showed pattern sensitivity alone.

#### Cortical Contrast Gain Control

- **Title:** Lack of cortical contrast gain control in human photosensitive epilepsy
- **PMID:** 10700258
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10700258/
- **Description:** Study demonstrating cortical mechanisms of contrast gain control are lacking or severely impaired in photosensitive subjects for low-medium temporal frequency stimuli.

## WCAG and W3C Accessibility Guidelines

### Primary W3C Resources

#### WCAG 2.1 Guideline 2.3: Seizures and Physical Reactions

- **URL:** https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG21/Understanding/seizures-and-physical-reactions.html
- **Principle:** "Do not design content in a way that is known to cause seizures or physical reactions."

#### WCAG 2.3.1: Three Flashes or Below Threshold (Level A)

- **URL:** https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG21/Understanding/three-flashes-or-below-threshold.html
- **Requirement:** Web pages do not contain anything that flashes more than three times in any one second period, or the flash is below the general flash and red flash thresholds.
- **Technical Threshold:** Combined area of flashes occupies no more than 0.006 steradians (25% of any 10-degree visual field)
- **Reference Screen:** 341 x 256 pixel block at 1024 x 768 resolution

#### WCAG 2.3.2: Three Flashes (Level AAA)

- **URL:** https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG21/Understanding/three-flashes.html
- **Requirement:** Web pages do not contain anything that flashes more than three times in any one second period, regardless of brightness or size.

#### WCAG 2.3.3: Animation from Interactions (Level AAA)

- **Requirement:** Motion animation triggered by interaction can be disabled unless essential.

### Key Technical Specifications

**Flash Thresholds:**
- Maximum: 3 flashes per second
- Most hazardous frequency range: 3-30 Hz (peak sensitivity 15-20 Hz)
- Extended range of concern: 3-70 Hz

**Red Flash Considerations:**
- Saturated red (660-720 nm wavelength) at higher risk
- Special test required for saturated red flashing
- Red color is more intense and more likely to trigger seizures

**Area Thresholds:**
- Maximum concurrent flash area: 25% of 10-degree visual field
- Reference: 341 x 256 pixels at 1024 x 768 screen

### Additional W3C Resources

- **Understanding WCAG 2.0 Seizure Guideline:** https://www.w3.org/TR/UNDERSTANDING-WCAG20/seizure.html
- **MDN Web Docs Accessibility Guide:** https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/Accessibility/Guides/Seizure_disorders

## Harding Test and Flash Analysis

### The Harding Flash and Pattern Analyser (FPA)

- **Official Website:** https://hardingtest.com/
- **Developer:** Cambridge Research Systems, Ltd. (with Prof. Graham Harding, Aston University)
- **Purpose:** Proprietary software analyzing video content for flashing and stationary patterns
- **Standards:** Implements Ofcom UK guidelines

### How the Harding Test Works

**Analysis Process:**
1. Examines video frames second-by-second
2. Analyzes for potentially provocative image sequences
3. Logs luminance flashes, red flashes, and spatial patterns
4. Compares against prescribed amplitude and frequency limits

**Technical Capabilities:**
- Supports SDI, composite, component, HDMI, and file-based inputs
- Analyzes resolutions up to 8K
- Available in tape-based and file-based versions

### Historical Background

- **1993:** Pot Noodles advertisement induced seizures in 3 UK viewers
- **1993:** ITC (predecessor to Ofcom) introduced guidelines
- **2001:** Harding FPA nominated for Royal Television Society technical innovation award

### Current Industry Usage

- Required for all UK television broadcasts
- All TV content must pass Automated PSE and QC test
- Programs failing test require re-editing (reducing flashes, color intensity)
- Modern alternatives: BATON, Vidchecker (replaced older FPA software)

### Related References

- **Wikipedia - Harding Test:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harding_test
- **Wikipedia - Photosensitive Epilepsy:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosensitive_epilepsy

## Safe Visual Design Guidelines

### Flash Frequency Guidelines

**Critical Thresholds:**
- Maximum safe: 3 flashes per second
- Most dangerous range: 15-20 Hz
- Extended risk range: 3-30 Hz (up to 60 Hz reported)

**Luminance Requirements (Epilepsy Foundation):**
- Danger threshold: >20 cd/m2 luminance
- Frequency: 3 Hz or higher
- Visual angle: >0.006 steradians

### Pattern Safety Guidelines

**Stripe Patterns:**
- Maximum 5 light-dark pairs if changing/oscillating
- Applies when pattern subtends >0.006 steradians
- Light stripe luminance >50 cd/m2
- Pattern displayed for >0.5 seconds

**Area Coverage:**
- Flashing content: <25% of screen area
- Static repeated patterns: <40% of screen area
- Moving repeated patterns: <25% of screen area

### Color Considerations

**Red Color Risk:**
- Wavelength 660-720 nm particularly hazardous
- Saturated red to/from transitions always risky regardless of luminance
- Red flashing requires additional testing

**Safe Color Practices:**
- Avoid fully saturated reds
- Avoid rapid red-blue alternation (especially at 12 Hz)
- Prefer desaturated colors for animations

### Content Creator Recommendations

**Essential Practices:**
1. Keep flashing below 3 per second
2. Avoid high-contrast geometric patterns (checkerboards, stripes)
3. Provide user controls for animations
4. Include clear warnings before potentially triggering content
5. Replace flashing GIFs with controllable video
6. Test with PEAT tool before publishing

**User Control Features:**
- Autoplay disabled by default
- Play/pause controls on all videos
- Animation disable option in accessibility settings
- Reduced motion media query support (`prefers-reduced-motion`)

### Testing Duration Considerations

- Sequences >5 seconds at threshold risk cumulative effects
- Even sub-threshold flashing can be problematic over extended periods
- ISO, Ofcom, ITU-R all mention cumulative risk

### Legal Disclaimer

**Important:** Never use term "epilepsy safe" - seizure risk cannot be completely eliminated. Using this term may result in legal liability.

### Design Resources

- **Adobe Design Accessibility Guide:** https://adobe.design/stories/design-for-scale/improving-product-accessibility-for-users-with-epilepsy
- **Game Accessibility Guidelines:** https://gameaccessibilityguidelines.com/avoid-flickering-images-and-repetitive-patterns/
- **MDN Web Accessibility Guide:** https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/Accessibility/Guides/Seizure_disorders

## Broadcast Standards

### UK - Ofcom Broadcasting Code

**Regulatory Framework:**
- **Rule 2.12:** "Television broadcasters must take precautions to maintain a low level of risk to viewers who have photosensitive epilepsy."
- **URL:** https://www.ofcom.org.uk/tv-radio-and-on-demand/broadcast-standards/section-two-harm-offence

**Technical Requirements:**
- Maximum 3 flashes per second
- Restricted area for flashing lights
- Restricted area for alternating patterns
- Testing via Harding FPA or equivalent (BATON, Vidchecker)

**Legacy Guidelines Document:**
- **URL:** https://www.ofcom.org.uk/siteassets/resources/documents/tv-radio-and-on-demand/broadcast-guidance/gn_flash.pdf

**Compliance History (2005-2021):**
- 30 instances of broadcasts exceeding PSE guidelines
- 80% of complaints submitted by viewers

### International - ITU-R BT.1702

**Current Version:** ITU-R BT.1702-3 (November 2023)
- **PDF:** https://www.itu.int/dms_pubrec/itu-r/rec/bt/R-REC-BT.1702-3-202311-I!!PDF-E.pdf
- **Original Version (2005):** https://www.itu.int/dms_pubrec/itu-r/rec/bt/r-rec-bt.1702-0-200502-i!!pdf-e.pdf

**Key Provisions:**
- Guidance for reduction of photosensitive epileptic seizures caused by television
- Safe limits for luminance changes
- Restricts flashes to no more than 3 per second
- Avoids frequencies between 5-30 Hz

**Compliance Requirement (from January 2019):**
- All TV programs must be tested using PSE algorithms per ITU-R BT.1702 revisions

### Japan - NHK/NAB Guidelines

**Historical Background:**
- **December 16, 1997:** Pokemon Episode 38 "Denno Senshi Porygon" caused seizures in 600+ children
- **Cause:** Rapid red-blue alternating flashes at 12 Hz
- Incident known as "Pokemon Shock" (ポケモンショック)

**Guidelines Developed:**
- Red flashing limited to 3 per second maximum
- Total red flash duration: 2 seconds maximum
- Flashing/flickering restricted if >50% of screen area

**Testing:**
- Harding Test website offers Japanese/NAB 2006 spec testing option

**On-Screen Warnings:**
- "Watch in well-lit room, sit far from TV" warnings
- Primarily shown with children's programming

### ISO Standards

**ISO 9241-391:2016**
- **Title:** Ergonomics of human-system interaction - Part 391: Requirements, analysis and compliance test methods for the reduction of photosensitive seizures
- **URL:** https://www.iso.org/standard/56350.html
- **Scope:** Requirements for reducing PSS while viewing electronic displays
- **Status:** New version (ISO/AWI 9241-391) in development

**Key Content:**
- Requirements applicable to image contents (device-independent)
- Addresses flashing, regular patterns, saturated red
- Includes viewing environment considerations (Annex D)
- References ITU-R BT.1702 and WCAG 2.0

### US - Section 508

- Screen flicker frequency restricted: >2 Hz and <55 Hz
- Federal agency websites must comply
- Enforcement: Rehabilitation Act

## Epidemiology and Prevalence

### General Population Statistics

| Metric | Rate | Source |
|--------|------|--------|
| General population prevalence | 1 in 4,000 | Multiple sources |
| Lifetime prevalence | 1 in 10,000 | Population studies |
| Annual incidence | ~1 per 100,000 | General population |
| Peak incidence (adolescents) | ~6 per 100,000 | Late adolescent age group |

### Prevalence Among People with Epilepsy

| Metric | Rate |
|--------|------|
| PPR on EEG | 2-14% of epilepsy patients |
| Clinical photosensitivity | ~2% of epilepsy patients |
| Ages 7-19 years | Up to 10% |
| PPR elicited by IPS | 3.5-4.3% (range 0.6-5.5%) |

### Demographics

**Age Distribution:**
- Peak onset: Puberty (ages 7-19)
- Highest risk age group: Late adolescence
- <25% of patients lose photosensitivity after twenties

**Gender Distribution:**
- Female predominance: 1.5-2x higher prevalence
- Female percentage in PPR studies: 67-72%
- Older patients (50+): 56% female (reduced predominance)

### Healthy Population EEG Studies

- Children (ages 1-15): 8.3% showed PPR (higher in girls, children >10)
- Military screening: 0.35-2.4% epileptiform abnormalities

## Neurological Mechanisms

### Visual Cortex Hyperexcitability

**Core Finding:**
The distinguishing feature of photosensitive individuals is intrinsic hyperexcitability of the visual cortex, predisposing to large-scale neuronal synchronization.

**TMS Studies:**
- Patients with photosensitivity: Lower phosphene threshold
- Patients with photosensitivity: Higher phosphene prevalence
- Biomarker potential: Phosphene threshold as excitability marker

**Reference:**
- "Visual cortex hyperexcitability in idiopathic generalized epilepsies with photosensitivity: a TMS pilot study" - PMID: 23518608

### Impaired Inhibitory Mechanisms

**Alpha Rhythm Dysfunction:**
- Reduced alpha-related inhibition in photosensitive epilepsy
- Lower BOLD deactivation when alpha power increases
- Affects occipital cortex, supplementary motor area, sensorimotor and premotor cortex

**Reference:**
- "Photosensitive epilepsy is associated with reduced inhibition of alpha rhythm generating networks" - PMC5837657

### Enhanced Neural Synchronization

**Key Findings:**
- Increased average power at stimulation frequency (occipital and frontal channels)
- Greater entrainment/locking of neuronal oscillation to visual stimulus
- Significantly increased phase clustering
- Impaired compensatory mechanisms preventing large-scale synchronization

**Reference:**
- "Cortical oscillations as seizure markers in photosensitive epilepsy" - PMC6752347

### Contrast Gain Control Deficits

- Abnormal contrast dependence of VEP amplitude and latency
- Affected: Low-medium temporal frequency, high luminance contrast
- Normal: Chromatic contrast (equiluminant red-green)

**Reference:**
- "Lack of cortical contrast gain control in human photosensitive epilepsy" - PMID: 10700258

### Genetic Factors

**Ion Channelopathies:**
- Voltage-gated chloride channelopathies
- Sodium channelopathies
- Contribute to hyperexcitability in IGE syndromes

**Novel Gene:**
- CHD2 implicated in photosensitive epilepsy
- Higher prevalence of unique CHD2 variants in patients vs. control population

### Seizure Generation Process

**Critical Factors:**
1. Sufficient rhythmic synchronization of neurons
2. Synchronization set up by flashing, flicker, oscillation, patterns
3. Impaired compensatory mechanisms in PSE patients
4. Visual cortex activation reaching critical neuronal population threshold

## 2025-2026 Additions

### PSE Hazard Analysis Tool Validation (2025)

**Title**: "Evaluating Conformance of Video Safety Tools for Photosensitive Epilepsy"
**Conference**: Springer LNCS (2025)
**DOI**: 10.1007/978-3-031-93848-1_7
**Key Finding**: Identifies validation gaps in current PSE hazard analysis tools
(PEAT, Harding FPA). Proposes new ground-truth video sequence generation for
validating and comparing PSE safety algorithms. Establishes testing paradigms
for conformance evaluation.
**Relevance**: Directly applicable to GATE-001 SEIZURE validator implementation.

### PEAT Status

The Photosensitive Epilepsy Analysis Tool (PEAT) from the Trace Center at University
of Maryland has been retired and is no longer officially supported. The Harding Flash
and Pattern Analyzer (FPA) is the successor tool used by YouTube, Vimeo, and broadcast
platforms.

### Platform-Level Adoption

As of 2025-2026:
- YouTube and Vimeo run Harding FPA during upload; warn or block failing videos
- Adobe is implementing seizure safety analysis into its creative tools
- Browser-level protections are being explored for CSS animations

### Web Accessibility Enforcement

WCAG 2.2 SC 2.3.1 (Level A) remains the primary standard:
- Max 3 general flashes per second
- Max 3 red flashes per second
- Danger zone: 15-20 Hz frequency range
- Pattern limits: max 5 light-dark pairs if oscillating
