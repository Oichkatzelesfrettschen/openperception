## 1. Gong And Hertle (2024)

Citation:
`Infantile Nystagmus Syndrome-Associated Inherited Retinal Diseases: Perspectives from Gene Therapy Clinical Trials.`

Local artifacts:

- `papers/downloads/nystagmus/2024_INS_Gene_Therapy_Clinical_Trials.html`
- `papers/downloads/nystagmus/2024_INS_Gene_Therapy_Clinical_Trials.txt`
- `papers/downloads/nystagmus/2024_INS_Gene_Therapy_Clinical_Trials.pdf.trace.html`

Granular findings:

- The review describes infantile nystagmus syndrome (INS) as the most prevalent
  form of nystagmus in infancy and childhood, with incidence around 1.7 per
  1000 live births.
- It states that approximately 90% of INS cases are associated with visual
  sensory disorders.
- The paper further argues that about 70% of INS cases are associated with
  inherited retinal diseases.
- It frames molecular diagnosis as critical for diagnostic clarity, management,
  and eligibility for emerging gene-based therapies and clinical trials.
- The review emphasizes the phenotypic and genotypic complexity of INS-linked
  retinal disease rather than treating nystagmus as a single uniform condition.
- It surveys AAV-based gene-therapy work as a fast-moving clinical context for
  several underlying retinal disorders linked with INS.

Project distillation:

- Repo guidance should avoid treating nystagmus as only an input-control issue;
  it often rides alongside deeper sensory and retinal conditions.
- Accessibility support for nystagmus should keep eye-tracking failure paths and
  motion-stable presentation options in view.
- When the repo references nystagmus clinically, it should distinguish
  idiopathic eye-movement disorder from broader IRD-associated cases.
